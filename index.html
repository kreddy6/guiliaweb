About

Giulia Sales (she/her) is a Brazilian contemporary dance artist based in Oakland. Originally from the periferia of São Vicente, São Paulo, her journey began at age seven through local social projects. This foundation paved the way for a professional career, leading her to major competitive stages across Brazil.

A 2022 graduate of the Alonzo King LINES Ballet Training Program, her practice now serves as a bridge to her roots, integrating the rhythmic complexity of Brazilian modalities into contemporary frameworks.

Since relocating to the United States, Giulia has performed with Bay Area companies including Robert Moses' KIN, Kristin Damrow & Company, and REYES Dance. Nationally, she has collaborated with artists such as Micaela G. Taylor and Mike Tyus.

Through her artistry, Giulia investigates a non-universal dance, reflecting the cultural, historical, and social contexts from which it emerges. She moves as a testament to her cultural journey.

Artistic Statement

I am a Brazilian artist and contemporary dancer from the periphery of São Vicente, on the coast of São Paulo. My trajectory is deeply shaped by territory, ancestry, and displacement. My dance training began through a social project that did more than just provide artistic education: it transformed my understanding of the body, presence, and collective identity.

Over time, my practice expanded from the Brazilian periphery to international stages in the United States, exposing me to new methodologies and techniques. This experience added refinement, autonomy, and adaptability to my work while keeping me rooted in community values: resilience, collectivity, and active listening. It is at this intersection of territories, cultures, and aesthetics that my artistic research is founded.

Being an identical twin permeates my creative process. Despite shared experiences, my twin sister and collaborator, Giovana, and I dance in distinct ways, raising questions about the relationship between individual essence and the collective environment in the development of bodily expression.

While research on peripheral corporeality exists, these perspectives are rarely combined with the twin experience. My research presents two peripheral bodies in dialogue, broadening discussions on identity, collectivity, and artistic creation in marginalized territories. My goal is to show an original corporeality that fuses individual and collective memory, moving beyond the traditional limits of creation and deepening the reflection on identity and territory.
